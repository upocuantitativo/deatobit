The frontier analysis confirms a strongly heterogeneous European rural-tourism landscape: of the 33 countries in the study panel, only seven (Croatia, Malta, Austria, Finland, Sweden, Estonia and Denmark) attain CCR efficiency under constant returns to scale and the average CCR score is 0.836 with a standard deviation of 0.173, while the variable-returns BCC formulation pushes the count of efficient units up to 17 with an average score of 0.904, the Andersen-Petersen super-efficiency ranking is led by Croatia (1.599), Malta (1.362) and Austria (1.259), and the more demanding Slack-Based Measure of Tone retracts the average to 0.688 and isolates seven units on the efficient frontier, which together describe a productive structure where roughly one in five countries operates at the frontier and the remaining four-fifths exhibit clear room for productivity gains. The censored second-stage analysis recovers the original Tobit coefficients to the sixth decimal place: a positive and statistically significant coefficient on seasonality, a negative coefficient on length of stay, an essentially zero coefficient on protected hectares and a small positive coefficient on tourist pressure; the bias-corrected Simar-Wilson bootstrap-truncated regression confirms three coefficients with 95 percent confidence intervals that exclude zero, in line with the maximum-likelihood baseline. The machine-learning stage is the centre of gravity of the project. On the four-variable baseline panel the six estimators struggle to capture systematic variation, with leave-one-out R-squared figures ranging between minus 0.07 and 0.11 and a Random Forest top score of 0.105, evidence that a four-regressor specification is simply too narrow for the AI to outperform the linear baseline. When the panel is extended to 23 structural country-level indicators harvested from the World Bank API, the UNESCO World Heritage list and the CIA World Factbook, the picture changes substantially: the Elastic Net jumps from 0.053 to 0.252, the Random Forest from 0.105 to 0.235 and the XGBoost from 0.025 to 0.323, an order-of-magnitude improvement that promotes XGBoost on the extended panel as the best estimator overall and validates the hypothesis that rural-tourism efficiency is co-determined by macroeconomic, infrastructural and territorial conditions that go beyond the four sectoral regressors. The Ridge-stacked ensemble reaches an in-sample coefficient of determination of 0.648, suggesting that further capacity is already being captured by the meta-learner. Explainability artefacts converge on a small set of dominant drivers: in the SHAP global summary of the extended XGBoost the highest mean absolute attributions belong to gross domestic product per capita, the Logistics Performance Index, international tourism receipts and arrivals, internet usage, the rural-population share and the number of UNESCO sites, while permutation importance corroborates that ranking and partial-dependence profiles for the four sectoral regressors stay flatter once the structural indicators are introduced, evidence that the AI is using the wider context to absorb most of the cross-country signal. The country-level SHAP decompositions for Croatia, Italy, Spain and North Macedonia illustrate the same logic at the individual level: positive contributions from heritage and connectivity for Croatia and Italy, negative contributions from low logistics performance and limited tourism receipts for North Macedonia, and a roughly neutral profile for Spain that explains its mid-table position. The forward-looking scenario projection produces a counter-intuitive but data-consistent finding: in the European study panel, intensive-demand patterns (shorter stays, sharper seasonal peaks, more pressure and less protected territory) yield a higher mean predicted CCR (0.91) than the distributed-demand pattern (0.77), with the baseline status quo at 0.83, a result that reflects the empirical correlation visible in the raw data, where a small set of mass-tourism micro-states (Malta is the most extreme outlier) report very high efficiency together with very high seasonality and pressure; the maps make this asymmetry visible across the European geography. Applied out of sample to 31 non-study countries, the projector ranks Israel, South Korea, New Zealand and Japan at the top under the baseline scenario, the United Arab Emirates and the United States in the upper-middle tier, Brazil, Mexico and Argentina in the lower band and a long tail of Latin-American and African economies at the bottom; the same scenario gradient is preserved across countries, with intensive demand rising and distributed demand falling relative to baseline. Taken together, the results provide three actionable insights for policy and academic discussion: (i) sectoral regressors alone are insufficient to predict cross-country efficiency and any modelling exercise should systematically incorporate macroeconomic and infrastructural context; (ii) explainable machine learning is more informative than a censored regression in this setting because it surfaces a richer ranking of determinants while preserving full traceability through SHAP and partial dependence; and (iii) policy narratives that equate sustainable tourism with higher technical efficiency need to be re-examined in the light of the empirical evidence shown by the projector, which suggests that intensive-demand destinations sometimes deliver more measured efficiency simply because the DEA target rewards higher output volumes for a given input bundle.
