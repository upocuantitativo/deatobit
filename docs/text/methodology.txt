The empirical strategy of this project unfolds in three sequential and complementary stages that are designed to be reproducible from end to end and to keep the analytical pipeline auditable at every intermediate step. In the first stage, the technical efficiency of the rural-tourism industry is estimated for the 33 European countries that make up the study panel. Each country is treated as a Decision Making Unit and four standard frontier programs are solved as linear programs through the high-performance HiGHS dual-simplex solver: the input-oriented constant-returns Charnes-Cooper-Rhodes formulation, the variable-returns Banker-Charnes-Cooper formulation, the Andersen-Petersen super-efficiency formulation that drops the evaluated DMU from the reference set so that fully efficient countries can still be ranked, and the non-radial Slack-Based Measure proposed by Tone, which penalises both input excesses and output shortfalls. Three productive inputs are used (number of beds, number of establishments and number of employees) together with two demand-side outputs (number of travellers and number of overnight stays), all of them sourced from Eurostat. The CCR efficiency score is taken as the headline target variable for the rest of the pipeline. In the second stage, the determinants of efficiency are explored through two complementary exercises. A right-censored Type-I Tobit is estimated by maximum likelihood with outer-product-of-gradient standard errors and a Simar-Wilson Algorithm-1 bootstrap-truncated regression with one thousand replications is computed as a bias-corrected cross-check. These regressions act as a transparent statistical baseline that produces interpretable coefficients on four sectoral regressors: seasonality, average length of stay, total protected hectares and tourist pressure. In the third and central stage, six machine-learning estimators are trained on the same target with the explicit goal of predicting and explaining cross-country variation in efficiency. The estimators are an Elastic Net with combined L1 and L2 penalty selected by 5-fold cross-validation, a Random Forest of 600 trees, a Gradient Boosting machine of 400 sequential trees, an XGBoost regressor with subsampling and L2 regularisation, a LightGBM histogram-based gradient booster and a Ridge meta-learner that stacks the five base estimators on out-of-fold predictions. All estimators are wrapped in standard-scaler pipelines and evaluated under leave-one-out cross-validation, the only sensible scheme given the small sample size. Two feature sets are systematically compared: a four-variable baseline that mirrors the original Tobit specification and an extended panel of 23 structural country-level indicators harvested from the World Bank API (gross domestic product per capita in current USD and PPP, gross domestic product growth, services and agriculture value-added, tertiary-school enrolment, internet usage, mobile-cellular subscriptions, forest area, agricultural land, terrestrial protected areas, hospital beds per thousand inhabitants, the Logistics Performance Index, international tourism receipts, international tourism arrivals, rural-population share and total population), the UNESCO World Heritage list and the CIA World Factbook (number of international airports). The resulting predictions are then opened with three complementary explainability tools: model-agnostic permutation importance, SHAP values computed with the TreeExplainer for the tree-based learners, and partial-dependence profiles for each of the four sectoral regressors. A country-level SHAP decomposition is also produced to illustrate which features push the prediction up or down for individual Decision Making Units. A unified comparative scoreboard ranks every estimator on a single metric and selects the best ML estimator as the one with the highest leave-one-out coefficient of determination on the extended panel. This best estimator is then used to project three forward-looking scenarios that perturb the four sectoral regressors symmetrically around the observed values: an intensive-demand scenario that simultaneously shortens the average stay by 15 percent, raises seasonality by 25 percent, raises tourist pressure by 20 percent and reduces protected hectares by 10 percent; a baseline scenario that holds every variable at its observed value; and a distributed-demand scenario that lengthens the stay by 20 percent, lowers seasonality by 25 percent, lowers tourist pressure by 15 percent and raises protected hectares by 10 percent. The three scenarios are rendered as Plotly choropleth maps over the European study panel and the same projector is also applied to a separate panel of 31 non-study countries spanning North America, Latin America, Asia, the Middle East, Oceania, Africa and non-EU Europe; the four sectoral regressors are imputed for those countries through ordinary-least-squares projection on macro-indicators that are common to both panels. The result is a fully reproducible pipeline made up of nine numbered scripts that together produce the tidy CSV inputs, the four DEA solutions, the censored regression coefficients, the World Bank-augmented panel, the trained ML pipelines, the bilingual figures, the choropleth maps, the out-of-sample country predictions and the final comparative scoreboard.
